Introduction:
This module, Identifying Main Idea and Supporting Details, focuses on developing learners’ listening skills through authentic, meaningful tasks. Listening is a vital macro skill that supports comprehension, communication, and critical thinking in both academic and real-world contexts.
Target Learner Profile
This module is designed for Junior High School students at the early–intermediate proficiency level in listening. These learners often recognize familiar words but struggle to identify the main idea and supporting details in spoken input. The module provides scaffolded tasks to help them progress toward upper–intermediate proficiency, where they can confidently summarize and organize information from authentic audio and video materials. These learners are situated in an academic classroom context and will engage with the module over a single lesson lasting about 60 minutes.
Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, you will be able to:
Identify the main ideas and supporting details in an audio;
Organize information using a graphic organizer; and
Demonstrate effective listening strategies while engaging with authentic spoken input.
Theoretical and Pedagogical Rationale
The design of this module is grounded in two key Second Language Acquisition (SLA) principles:
Krashen’s Input Hypothesis (i+1): Learners are exposed to authentic listening materials slightly above their current level. Visual scaffolds, guided prompts, and structured tasks ensure that input remains comprehensible while still challenging.
Task-Based Language Teaching (TBLT): Learners engage in meaningful, real-world tasks such as completing graphic organizers and reflecting on safety tips. Tasks are sequenced from pre-task to while-task to post-task, ensuring scaffolding and progression.
Together, these principles ensure that learners not only receive comprehensible input but also use listening strategies in purposeful tasks, strengthening both comprehension and communicative competence.

Reading
A main idea is the most important thought about a text. The main idea is what the whole text is mostly about. It usually tells about the person, thing or idea.
The supporting details explain, strengthen, or give examples of the main idea. They provide facts, reasons, or descriptions that help us understand the central thought more clearly.

We use main ideas and supporting details to make sense of texts, to organize our own writing, and even in everyday conversations. The main idea introduces the topic (e.g., “I got a new video game this weekend”), while supporting details provide reasons or explanations (e.g., “I want to go to the pizza place because the food is good and they have arcade games”).

The following steps will help you find the main idea and identify the key sentences and supporting details.
Step 1: Identify the topic. To identify the topic, ask yourself, "What is this about?" The answer provides the topic. It does not need to be a complete sentence, it is usually no longer than two or three words.
Step 2: Identify what the author is saying about the topic. This answers the question, "What does the author want me to know about the topic?" Again, it does not need to be a sentence and is generally five to seven words long.
Step 3: Identify details that support or explain the main idea. Ask yourself, "What else in the text helps me to understand the author's main idea?" "Does this fact, example, or explanation help support the main idea?

Key Strategies to Apply While Reading or Listening
Spot the topic - Keep your answer short and focused.
Separate the main point from examples - Recognize which statement carries the central idea and which ones only support it.
Notice emphasis - Pay attention to tone, pace, or repeated words — these often highlight the main idea.
Take active notes - Summarize in your own words instead of copying everything.
Check connections - Make sure each detail clearly supports the main idea; if not, it’s extra information.
